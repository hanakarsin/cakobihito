Opinion: Social Media Infodemic Poses a Challenge to COVID-19 Vaccine Uptake in Kenya

Kenya continues to receive vaccine donations from various countries, with UK being the latest to donate over 407,000 COVID-19 vaccine doses.

The doses will boost the ongoing vaccination campaign that has seen 2,101,403 people receive at least one dose of COVID-19 vaccine and 74800 people receive the second dose for those receiving the AstraZeneca vaccine.

Those vaccinated so far represents 1.4% of the total population.

According to the Ministry of Health (MOH), Nairobi county is in the lead with 8.2% of its population fully vaccinated, followed by Nyeri county 6.2%, Uasin Gishu with 4.6%, Laikipia 4.1%, Nakuru 3.9%, while Marsabit is the least with 0.3%, Garissa and Tana River at 0.4 % of the population fully vaccinated.

Despite the effort to achieve 10 million fully vaccinated adult individuals by December 2021, social media infodemic positions risk achieving the target.

What is Infodemic?

Infodemic is a blend of the words "information" and "epidemic" and refers to widely spreading both accurate and inaccurate information about a disease.

According to World Health Organisation, infodemic is too much information, including false or misleading information in digital and physical environments during a disease outbreak.

WHO explained that infodemic causes confusion and risk-taking behaviours that can harm the health of individuals and ignite mistrust in health authorities and undermine the public health response.

Any information whose sources have not been approved can mislead the public and interfere with the government goals towards fighting any pandemic, not necessarily COVID-19.

Different studies have recommended that it is essential to rely on information from endorsed sources like the Centre of Disease Prevention and Control(CDC), World Health Organization(WHO) and the State Health departments for information rather than relying on social media.

James Oluoch, 62, a businessman in Kakamega town, still believes the vaccine is not safe for humans though he does not cite a credible source informing his false conclusion.

“I have seen people online saying that. Many people said early that the speed of making the vaccine is not good. Those who have gone for the jab have been saying they feel sick and that has not motivated me to go for it,” said Oluoch.

Through the conversation, he cited online and TikTok videos he received through his Whatsapp application as sources of his information concerning the vaccines.

Upon watching some of the clips viewed by James, I noticed that they were created by individuals who are not medical experts trying to show how a silver coin is stacked at the place where the jab is introduced in the human body.

Social media platforms play a big role in disseminating information and are also key in determining how people receive and perceive information.

Social media sites are generally websites and applications that enable users to create and share content or to participate in social networking.

COVID-19 Propaganda

Since communication is sharing information, ideas, thoughts, and feelings, social media cannot miss any dialogue or discussion concerning communication versus propaganda.

Propaganda has a simple definition of disseminating information, facts, arguments, rumours, half-truths or lies with a major purpose of influencing public opinion and mostly evokes emotions.

In communication, mass media is perceived as a system for spreading and relaying information and messages to the public and also plays a key role in amusing, entertaining and informing individuals with rules and values that position them in social structure.

Therefore, in the world of communication, propaganda is understood as a tool that creates conflicts among society's differing classes.

Social media has been cited as a notorious platform due to the increase of spreading infodemic concerning COVID-19 transmission and vaccination, thus posing a major challenge towards the fight of the pandemic in Kenya.

Anyone anywhere can tap an app and access information concerning any subject without any restriction from the so-called gatekeepers who should determine and validate the information that audiences receive.

TikTok app, which is on the lead for generating content to the viewers, was first launched in China in September 2016 by Douyin, who developed the app within 200 days. Within 1 year, it had attracted 200 million users and had over one billion videos viewed every day.

In Kenya, apart from TikTok being a business entity through content creation, it is also a tool from which infodemic and mimicry, which poses a challenge towards the fight of the pandemic, can be spread.

As postulated by Brian Dean's publication on the Backlink website, global statistics show that as of January 2021, the current percentage of people using social media was 50.64% of the world's total population.

In Kenya, according to the Digital 2021 report, there were approximately 11 million social media users in Kenya in January 2021.

The report also shows that the number of social media users in the country increased by 2.2 million that is 25%, between 2020 and 2021; hence the number of social media users in Kenya was equivalent to 20.2% of the total population in January 2021.

From these statistics, the social media infodemic has played a major role in fighting the pandemic in Kenya and globally.

Evidence shows that mobile phone connections in Kenya as of January 2021 were 59.24 million, increasing by 11% compared to January 2020.

Also, Internet World Stats statistics on internet usage says Kenya recorded 85.2% of internet users among the total population of 54.9 million people as of 31st December 2020, showing that a good number of citizens can access online infodemic.

Moreover, the use of social media has been associated with the younger generation, which is true. However, this younger generation disseminates the infodemic to the old generation who cannot access online information, influencing vaccine uptake.

The American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry (AACAP) survey shows that 90% of teenagers aged between 17 and 18 years have used social media.

At least 75% of teenagers reported having at least one active social media profile, and 51% report visiting a social media site daily. In addition, two-thirds of teens have their own mobile devices with internet capabilities.

From the highlighted statistics, we can conclude that surely social media is a source of infodemic.

Therefore, the government has a responsibility to support in controlling the information disseminated online through partnerships with social media sites and applications developers.

Relevant authorities also need to proactively develop education programmes with relevant institutions to empower public officers and the masses’ understanding of proper social media usage.

This will increase the number of knowledgeable gatekeepers who will communicate truthfully and with the backup of experts’ input, thus supporting good causes like COVID-19 vaccine uptake.
